  \begin{Ej}
    We will explore a surface through contour lines. 
    \begin{itemize}
      \item The vertical contour lines are all of the form \un{$x^2+y^2=9$}.
      \item Horizontal contour lines across the $y$ direction are of the form \un{$x^2=9-a^2$} with $-3\leq a\leq 3$.
      These lines live in planes parallel to the $xz$-plane.
    \end{itemize}
    A key fact is that this shape is \textit{symmetric across the origin}. Do the following:
    \begin{enumerate}
      \item Draw an example of the vertical contour lines on a plane. What figure are they?
      \item Draw the horizontal contour lines at the values of $a=-3$ and $a=0$. Describe the figures and mention what happens for values $-3<a<0$.
      \item Draw a diagram in 3 dimensions joining both sets of contour lines. What is the surface that we obtain?
    \end{enumerate}
    \end{Ej}

    \begin{Ej}
      Consider the expressions 
      $$z=(x+y)(x-y),\quad \left\lbrace
      \begin{aligned}
        &x=r\cos(\te)\\
        &y=r\sin(\te)
      \end{aligned}
      \right.$$
      Calculate the partial derivatives $\pdv{z}{r}$ and $\pdv{z}{\te}$.
    \end{Ej}
    \begin{ptcb}
      We may write this expressions as two functions:
      $$z=f(x,y)=(x+y)(x-y),\word{and}G(r,\te)=(r\cos(\te),r\sin(\te)).$$
      The task is then to find $D(F(G))_x$ which by the chain rule is 
      $$D(F(G))_{(r,\te)}=D(f)_{G(r,\te)}\. DG_{(r,\te)}=\nb f_{G(r,\te)}\. JG_{(r,\te)}.$$
      The derivatives of our functions are
      $$\nb f_{(x,y)}=(f_x,f_y)=(2x,-2y)\word{and}JG=\twobytwo{G_{1x}}{G_{1y}}{G_{2x}}{G_{2y}}=\twobytwo{\cos(\te)}{-r\sin(\te)}{\sin(\te)}{r\cos(\te)}.$$
      Evaluating the gradient at $G(r,\te)$ we get 
      $$\nb f_{G(r,\te)}=(2(r\cos(\te)),-2(r\sin(\te))).$$
      The product of this gradient with the Jacobian will give us the desired result:
      \begin{align*}
        \nb f_{G(r,\te)}\.JG&=(2r\cos(\te),-2r\sin(\te))\twobytwo{\cos(\te)}{-r\sin(\te)}{\sin(\te)}{r\cos(\te)}\\
        &=\twobyone{(2r\cos(\te),-2r\sin(\te))\.(\cos(\te),\sin(\te))}{(2r\cos(\te),-2r\sin(\te))\.(-r\sin(\te),r\cos(\te))}\\
        &=\twobyone{2r\cos^2(\te)-2r\sin^2(\te)}{-2r^2\sin(\te)\cos(\te)-2r^2\sin(\te)\cos(\te)}\\
        &=\twobyone{2r\cos(2\te)}{-2r^2\sin(2\te)}
      \end{align*}
      So from this $\pdv{z}{r}=2r\cos(2\te)$ and $\pdv{z}{\te}=-2r^2\sin(2\te)$. The answers could've also been left as the second-to-last line.
    \end{ptcb}
